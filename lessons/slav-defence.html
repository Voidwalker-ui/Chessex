The Slav Defence

The Slav is the most solid reply to the Queen's Gambit. Unlike 2...e6, Black supports the d5 pawn with the c-pawn, keeping the light-squared bishop free and avoiding the 'bad bishop' problem that can haunt the Queen's Gambit Declined for the entire game.

The Slav after 2...c6. Black supports the d5 pawn with the c-pawn instead of e6, keeping the light-squared bishop's diagonal open. A single move, but it defines the entire strategic character.

The key difference from the QGD

In the Queen's Gambit Declined (2...e6), Black blocks the c8 bishop for many moves. In the Slav (2...c6), Black keeps c8 open by supporting d5 with the c-pawn. The light-squared bishop then becomes an active piece, often emerging to f5 or g4.

Main variations

The Slav splits into several major systems based on how the game continues:

  • Slav Accepted (3.cxd5 cxd5): temporarily gives up the centre for development.
  • Semi-Slav (3.Nf3 Nf6 4.Nc3 e6): combines Slav and QGD structures; leads to the wildly complicated Botvinnik and Moscow Variations.
  • Exchange Slav: White trades on d5 early, leading to quiet symmetrical positions.

Strategic themes

Black's main plans involve developing the c8 bishop actively, breaking in the centre with ...e5 or ...dxc4 followed by ...b5, and using the half-open c-file for rook activity.

White typically tries to exploit the slight space advantage gained by the d4-c4 pawn duo and open lines in the centre.

Who should play it

Players who want a theoretically sound and well-understood defence against 1.d4 that avoids the 'bad bishop' problems of the QGD and provides genuine counterplay.
