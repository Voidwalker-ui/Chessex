The Rules of Chess

Six pieces, 64 squares, and a handful of rules that have remained essentially unchanged since the 15th century. Understanding these rules completely is the foundation of everything else.

The objective

The goal of chess is to deliver checkmate: a position where the opponent's King is under attack (in check) and has no legal move to escape. The game ends the moment checkmate is delivered — the King is never actually captured.

If you are in check, your only legal moves are those that resolve the check: moving the King to a safe square, blocking the attacking piece with another piece, or capturing the attacking piece.

Turn order and legal moves

White always moves first. Players alternate one move at a time. A move is any legal action: moving a piece, capturing a piece, castling, or promoting a pawn.

  • You must make a move — passing is not allowed.
  • You cannot move into check or leave your King in check.
  • If you have no legal moves and are NOT in check, the position is stalemate — an immediate draw.

Special moves

Four special moves exist beyond normal piece movement:

  • Castling: King and Rook swap, putting the King behind pawns and connecting the Rooks.
  • En passant: A pawn captures a pawn that just advanced two squares past it.
  • Promotion: A pawn reaching the back rank becomes any piece (almost always a Queen).
  • None of these can be performed through check.

How the game ends

The game ends in one of three outcomes:

  • Checkmate: one player's King has no escape. That player loses.
  • Resignation: a player concedes before checkmate, acknowledging the position is hopeless.
  • Draw: either by agreement, stalemate, insufficient material (e.g. only two Kings remain), threefold repetition of the same position, or fifty consecutive moves without a pawn move or capture.

Start here, go anywhere

These rules take ten minutes to learn and a lifetime to master. Once they are automatic, every other lesson on Chess-X builds on them.
