King and Pawn Endings

King and pawn endings are the bedrock of endgame theory. Every concept — opposition, key squares, the square rule — is clearest here. Master these endings and you will have a framework that applies to rook, bishop, and queen endings too.

Key concept: opposition and key squaresTurning point: can the pawn queen?Difficulty: Beginner–Intermediate
♚ ♙ ♔ ♖

King, Pawn vs King. The central question: can the White King escort the pawn to the queening square? Answer depends on the opposition — which King is in front of the other.

Opposition

Two Kings face each other in 'opposition' when they are separated by exactly one square on the same file, rank, or diagonal. The player who does NOT have to move has the opposition. Having the opposition forces the enemy King backward.

In king-pawn endings, opposition is often the entire game. Gaining it allows the attacking King to advance; losing it lets the defending King hold the draw.

Key squares

For every pawn, there are specific squares on the board — called key squares — such that if the attacking King reaches one of them, the pawn promotes regardless of where the defending King stands.

For a pawn on e4, the key squares are d6, e6, and f6. The attacker's entire endgame plan is to get the King to one of these squares.

Drawn pawn endings

Not all king-pawn vs king endings are wins. A rook pawn (a- or h-pawn) is the most common drawing exception: because there is no room to outflank the defending King on one side, the defender can often hold the draw by placing the King in front of the pawn.

If the attacking King cannot gain the opposition and reach the key squares, the result is a draw by stalemate or repetition.

The test

Before trading into a pawn ending, calculate whether your King can reach the key squares. Many games are lost by assuming an endgame is 'easily won' without checking the opposition.
