How to Use Tactics Puzzles (Most Players Do It Wrong)

Tactics puzzles are the most popular chess training tool, but the way most players use them produces far less improvement than it should. The difference between solving puzzles and actually getting better at tactics is entirely in the method.

Category: Training methodsLevel: All levelsEstimated reading time: 8 minutes

A blank board. Before the position matters, the method matters more.

The problem with passive solving

Most players open a puzzle set and solve one puzzle after another, as fast as possible. They get the right answer, feel good, and move on. The problem: speed-solving tactical puzzles trains pattern recognition in ideal conditions — perfectly framed positions with a clear starting point. Real games don't announce themselves as 'tactics puzzles.'

The real skill is not solving the puzzle once you've identified a tactic is there. It's identifying, in a position with 40 legal moves and no prompt, that a tactic is available.

The correct method

  • Before looking at the board, identify the side to move and both players' most active pieces.
  • Write (or say aloud) the candidate moves before calculating — not after.
  • Look for checks, captures, and threats, in that order.
  • After finding the solution, look for the defender's best resistance. Does the tactic still work?
  • After the session, review puzzles you got wrong without immediately revealing the answer — spend time with the confusion.

Volume vs. difficulty

Many coaches recommend fewer, harder puzzles over more easier ones. A puzzle that takes ten minutes to solve trains calculation and pattern recognition far more deeply than ten one-minute puzzles. Once a level becomes easy, move up.

The goal is to be slightly uncomfortable — at the edge of your calculation ability, not comfortably solving patterns you already know.

Connecting puzzles to games

After every game you play, look for the moments where a tactic existed — whether you played it or not. The game position is the real test. The puzzle set is the training ground. Treat one as preparation for the other.

The discipline

Solve fewer puzzles, more slowly and carefully. The discomfort of not knowing the answer immediately is exactly where the improvement happens.
