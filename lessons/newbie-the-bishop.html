The Bishop

The Bishop is a long-range diagonal piece. Each player starts with two Bishops — one on light squares and one on dark squares — and they never leave their colour. Knowing which colour your Bishop lives on shapes your entire strategy.

Movement: any number of squares diagonallyPoint value: ~3 points (a pair is worth slightly more)Each Bishop stays on its starting colour all game
♗

The Bishop (♗) on d5. It controls both diagonals — 13 squares from this central position. It can never reach the squares of the opposite colour.

How the Bishop moves

The Bishop slides any number of squares diagonally. It cannot move horizontally or vertically, and it cannot jump over pieces. Because diagonals never change colour, a Bishop on a light square can only ever reach light squares — it is permanently colour-bound.

Good Bishop vs. Bad Bishop

A Bishop is 'good' when it is not blocked by its own pawns. If your pawns are fixed on light squares and your Bishop is also on light squares, it has no room to operate — this is called a 'bad Bishop'.

The classic principle: place your pawns on the opposite colour to your Bishop so the Bishop has clear diagonals to work with.

The Bishop pair

Owning both Bishops while your opponent has lost one or both of theirs is called 'the Bishop pair'. With an open board and no pawns blocking the diagonals, the Bishop pair controls colours across the entire board and is often considered a small but lasting advantage.

Bishop vs. Knight

Bishops prefer open positions with long diagonals. Knights prefer closed positions where their ability to jump over pieces gives them an advantage bishops lack. This trade-off is one of the most important positional decisions in chess.

Colour matters

Always be aware of which colour your Bishop is on. Many endgames are drawn or won based solely on whether the remaining Bishop can reach the queening square.
