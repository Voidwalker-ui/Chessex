Expanded Article

A Najdorf position of maximum sharpness — and the young Frenchman chose the most aggressive path through it.

The Candidates Tournament in Toronto produced no more violent game than round nine's encounter between Nepomniachtchi and Firouzja. Both players had entered the round with winning ambitions; only one left satisfied. Firouzja, playing Black in a Sicilian Najdorf, uncorked a piece sacrifice on move 22 that had all the hallmarks of deep preparation. The rook was surrendered on c3, the pawn structure shattered, and suddenly White's centre — seemingly dominant just five moves earlier — became the source of a lethal attack rather than the engine of victory.

Nepomniachtchi spent 41 minutes on his 24th move. He found the best defence available. It was not enough. Firouzja's attack moved with the efficiency of a prepared variation — every piece reaching its optimal square in sequence, every defensive resource pre-calculated and dismissed. By move 38, resignation came quietly, with three pawns left on the board and no path to survival.

The game is destined for the anthologies. Its move-22 sacrifice joins a lineage of Najdorf brilliancies stretching back through Fischer, Tal, and Polugaevsky — attacks so sharp that even engines require extended analysis time to confirm their soundness.

Critical Lesson

The practical value of this article is the connection between decision and consequence. Identify the moment where the plan becomes clear, then ask what earlier move made that plan possible.

  • What changed in the pawn structure?
  • Which piece improved or became permanently bad?
  • Did the winning idea come from calculation, technique, preparation, or psychology?
  • What position type should you now try to reach in your own games?
