Core Idea

Black pins White's knight before it can dominate the centre, attacking the pawn structure rather than fighting directly for e4. One of the most theoretically rich openings ever devised. Nimzo-Indian Defence is best learned as a set of plans rather than a list of moves. The key is to understand what the first pawn structure is asking your pieces to do, which files should open, and which endgames you are likely to reach if the middlegame simplifies.

1. d4 Nf6 2. c4 e6 3. Nc3 Bb4

Main Plans

Plan for Black

  • Challenge White's centre before it becomes mobile.
  • Keep piece activity high enough that the space disadvantage never becomes permanent.
  • Choose pawn breaks that open files for counterplay instead of defending passively.

What White Wants

  • White usually tries to use extra space to restrict your pieces.
  • Be ready for early central expansion and direct kingside pressure.
  • If the centre locks, find the correct flank break before White does.

Variation Map

Use the branches below as landmarks. Do not memorise them as isolated move strings; connect each one to a pawn break, a weak square, or a piece trade.

  • Classical (4.e3): Qs-side build
  • Rubinstein (4.e3 0-0): Solid structure
  • Sämisch (4.a3): Force the bishop pair

Common Mistakes

  • Playing the opening quickly without naming the middlegame plan.
  • Moving the same piece twice while the rest of the army is undeveloped.
  • Trading the active bishop or knight just because the capture is available.
  • Ignoring the endgame structure the opening is creating.

Practice Drill

Replay ten model games in this opening. Pause after move ten and write a one-sentence plan for both sides. Then jump to the endgame and ask which opening decisions still mattered.
