The Pawn

Pawns are the soul of chess. They cannot move backwards. Every decision you make about them is permanent, and those permanent decisions define the pawn structure — the skeleton around which the entire game is built.

How the Pawn moves

A Pawn advances one square forward. From its starting rank (rank 2 for White, rank 7 for Black), it may optionally advance two squares on its first move. It cannot move backwards under any circumstances.

Pawns capture differently than they move: they take pieces one square diagonally forward, never straight ahead.

En passant

If a pawn advances two squares from its starting rank and lands beside an opposing pawn, the opposing pawn may capture it as if it had only moved one square. This special capture is called en passant (French for 'in passing') and must be exercised immediately on the very next move or the right is lost.

Promotion

When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board (rank 8 for White, rank 1 for Black), it must be promoted — replaced immediately by any piece except a King. Almost always, players promote to a Queen. Promotion is often the decisive moment in pawn endgames.

Pawn structure

The arrangement of pawns on the board is called the pawn structure. Since pawns cannot retreat, every pawn move is irreversible. Doubled pawns (two on the same file), isolated pawns (no friendly pawns on adjacent files), and passed pawns (no enemy pawns blocking or flanking their advance) are the three most important structural features to understand.

Grandmaster advice

Study your pawn structure before moving pieces. The pawns determine where your pieces belong — they frame the positions your other pieces must work within.
