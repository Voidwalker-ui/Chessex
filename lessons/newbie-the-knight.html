The Knight

The Knight is the only piece that jumps over others. It moves in an L-shape — two squares in one direction and one square perpendicular — and lands on a square of the opposite colour each time. Its unpredictability makes it one of the trickiest pieces to face.

How the Knight moves

The Knight moves in an L-shape: two squares along a rank or file, then one square perpendicular to that. From a central square, it has eight possible destinations. From a corner square, it has only two. This edge penalty is why centralising Knights is so important.

Crucially, the Knight jumps — any pieces between the Knight and its destination are ignored. This makes it the only piece that can be deployed when the position is still blocked.

Always lands on the opposite colour

Every Knight move changes the colour of the square it occupies. A Knight on a light square always moves to a dark square. This means it takes exactly two moves to return to a square of the same colour, and an odd number of moves to reach a square of the opposite colour.

Knight outposts

A Knight is most powerful on an 'outpost' — a square in the opponent's half of the board where it cannot be attacked by enemy pawns. A Knight anchored on d5 or e5, supported by a pawn and safe from eviction, exerts enormous pressure on surrounding squares.

Knight vs. Bishop

Knights thrive in closed positions where pawns block the board and long-range pieces lose their effectiveness. Bishops thrive in open positions with clear diagonals. The decision to keep or trade a Knight for a Bishop is one of the fundamental strategic choices in chess.

The trick

A Knight attacks squares it cannot see from where it stands. Always check knight forks — a Knight attacking King and Queen at once is the most common tactical shot at club level.
