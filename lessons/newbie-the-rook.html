The Rook

The Rook is a long-range powerhouse. It controls entire ranks and files from any square on the board, and in the endgame — where open lines are plentiful — it often becomes the decisive force.

Movement: any number of squares along a rank or filePoint value: ~5 pointsSpecial move: castling with the King
♖

The Rook (♖) on d5. It controls the entire d-file (up and down) and the entire 5th rank (left and right) — 14 squares in total from this central position.

How the Rook moves

The Rook slides any number of squares horizontally or vertically. It cannot move diagonally. The path must be clear — it cannot jump over other pieces. From any square on an open board, the Rook controls exactly 14 squares regardless of where it stands.

Open files and 7th rank

A Rook on an open file — one with no pawns blocking the path — is extremely strong. Two Rooks on the same open file ('doubled Rooks') are even stronger, creating unstoppable battery along that column.

Reaching the 7th rank (or 2nd rank for Black) is a major goal: from there the Rook attacks the opponent's undeveloped pawns and hems in the enemy King.

Rook in the endgame

In the endgame, the Rook is often the dominant piece. With pawns as the primary material, the Rook's ability to attack and defend entire files and ranks makes it far superior to bishops or knights in many positions.

The basic principle: put your Rook behind a passed pawn — your own or your opponent's. Behind your own pawn, it pushes it forward. Behind the opponent's passed pawn, it stops it from queening.

Castling

The Rook participates in castling — the only move in chess that moves two pieces at once. The Rook jumps over the King to the adjacent square as the King steps two squares toward the Rook. Castling connects the Rooks and activates them quickly.

Key idea

Rooks need open lines. If pawns are blocking your Rooks all game, you are not using your heaviest artillery.
