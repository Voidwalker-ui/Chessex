The King

The King is the most important piece on the board. It cannot be traded. When it has no safe moves and is under attack, the game ends. Understanding how the King moves is where all chess begins.

How the King moves

The King can move exactly one square in any direction: horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. That gives it up to eight possible destinations from an open central square, and fewer from the edge of the board, where some destinations fall off the board entirely.

The most important rule in chess

The King must never move into a square attacked by an enemy piece. A position where the King is under direct attack is called check. When the King is in check, the player must immediately resolve the threat — either by moving the King, blocking the attack, or capturing the attacking piece.

If none of those options are available, the position is checkmate and the game is over. The player whose King is in checkmate has lost.

Castling — the King's only special move

Once per game, the King may perform a special two-piece move called castling. The King slides two squares toward a Rook on the same rank, and that Rook jumps over to land on the square the King crossed.

  • Neither the King nor the Rook involved may have moved previously.
  • The King may not be in check at the start, pass through, or land on an attacked square.
  • All squares between King and Rook must be empty.

Opening and endgame roles

In the opening, the King is a vulnerability. Getting it to safety — usually by castling — is one of the highest priorities in the first ten moves.

In the endgame, once most pieces are off the board, the King transforms into an active and powerful fighting piece. Centralising the King and advancing it toward the enemy pawns is one of the most important endgame skills.

Remember

Every rule in chess exists to protect, threaten, or deploy the King. It is the reason the game exists.
