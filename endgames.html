Endgame Mastery

Endgames

Capablanca advised studying the endgame first. It is here that technique separates the good from the great — and where every pawn carries the weight of a potential queen.

King & Pawn Endgames

K+P Endgame

The Opposition Beginner

When two kings face each other with one square between them, the player who does not have to move holds "the opposition." This concept governs all king and pawn endgames.

Key concept: Direct & distant opposition
K+P Endgame

The Square Rule Beginner

Can the defending king catch a passed pawn running for promotion? The "square rule" — drawing a diagonal square from the pawn — tells you in an instant.

Key concept: Pawn races
K+P Endgame

Corresponding Squares Advanced

A deep concept in complex king and pawn endgames. Corresponding squares define a hidden geometry that determines who can access which key squares first.

Key concept: Zugzwang & mapping

Rook Endgames

Rook Endgame

Lucena Position Intermediate

The most important winning technique in rook endgames. The stronger side uses the "bridge-building" method to escort the pawn to promotion against the defending rook.

Technique: Bridge building
Rook Endgame

Philidor Position Intermediate

The essential drawing technique. The defending rook cuts the opposing king off on the sixth rank — and switches to giving checks from behind once the pawn advances.

Technique: Back-rank defence
Rook Endgame

Rook Behind Passed Pawn Beginner

A principle so important it bears memorising: the rook belongs behind a passed pawn — whether yours or your opponent's. Always. No exceptions.

Principle: Tarrasch's rule
Rook Endgame

Cutting Off the King Intermediate

One of the most effective techniques in rook endgames. By placing the rook on a file or rank, you deny the defending king access to the winning zone entirely.

Technique: Rank/file cutoff
Rook Endgame

Rook vs. Two Pawns Advanced

Two connected passed pawns on the sixth rank typically defeat a lone rook. Passed pawns on the fifth are often held. Knowing the boundary matters enormously in practice.

Technique: Pawn coordination
Rook Endgame

Saavedra Position Advanced

A miraculous study that stunned the chess world. From apparent equality, White weaves a web of zugzwang and underpromotion to secure an unlikely victory.

Technique: Underpromotion

Minor Piece Endgames

Bishop Endgame

Opposite-Coloured Bishops Intermediate

Notorious for their drawing tendencies. When each side's bishop controls different-coloured squares, material advantages melt away — a saving grace for the worse side.

Concept: Drawing fortress
Knight Endgame

Knight vs. Rook Pawn Intermediate

A knight cannot stop a rook pawn — especially an a- or h-pawn — from promoting if the defending king is too far away. This seemingly simple truth saves and costs countless games.

Concept: The draw zone
Mixed Endgame

Bishop vs. Knight Advanced

Open positions favour the bishop; closed ones favour the knight. A player who understands this in the middlegame can steer the game to a winning endgame structure.

Concept: Pawn structure

"To improve at chess, you should in the first instance study the endgame."

— José Raúl Capablanca

The 7 Endgame Laws

I. Activate your king immediately.
II. Place rooks behind passed pawns.
III. Create a passed pawn when possible.
IV. Advance the outside passed pawn first.
V. In rook endgames, cut off the enemy king.
VI. Understand the opposition and use it.
VII. Know the drawn and won positions by heart.
