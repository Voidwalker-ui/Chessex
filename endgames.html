Endgame Mastery

Endgames

Capablanca advised studying the endgame first. It is here that technique separates the good from the great — and where every pawn carries the weight of a potential queen.

King & Pawn Endgames

K+P Endgame

The Opposition Beginner

When two kings face each other with one square between them, the player who does not have to move holds "the opposition." This concept governs all king and pawn endgames.

Key Squares Intermediate

Each pawn has a set of "key squares" — the squares the king must reach to guarantee promotion. For a pawn on e5, those squares are d7, e7, and f7. King activation begins with identifying them.

Trebuchet Advanced

A mutual zugzwang in king and pawn endgames where both sides have an equal pawn. Whichever king is forced to move loses. A position of absolute mutual dependency — and great elegance.

A passed pawn on the flank acts as a decoy, drawing the enemy king away from the main battle while your own king invades on the other side. A strategically decisive weapon at all levels.

Queen Endgames

Queen Endgame

Queen vs. Rook Advanced

Theoretically won for the stronger side, but practically very difficult — the defending rook constantly gives checks and sets up stalemate traps. Requires systematic triangulation and king centralisation. Studied by Philidor himself.

Technique: Philidor's method

Queen Endgame

Queen vs. Pawn on 7th Intermediate

A queen normally defeats a passed pawn — except when that pawn is on the 7th rank, supported by its king, and is a bishop's or rook's pawn. The resulting fortress draws are essential knowledge for competitive players.

Technique: Forced stalemate / draw

Queen Endgame Principles Intermediate

Queen endgames are among the most complex and double-edged in chess. The stronger side should centralise the queen, activate the king, and avoid perpetual checks. Passed pawns become queens — so counting them matters.

Knight vs. Rook Pawn Intermediate

A knight cannot stop a rook pawn — especially an a- or h-pawn — from promoting if the defending king is too far away. This seemingly simple truth saves and costs countless games.

The 7 Endgame Laws

I. Activate your king immediately.
II. Place rooks behind passed pawns.
III. Create a passed pawn when possible.
IV. Advance the outside passed pawn first.
V. In rook endgames, cut off the enemy king.
VI. Understand the opposition and use it.
VII. Know the drawn and won positions by heart.
