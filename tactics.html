Tactical Patterns

Tactics

Tactics are the vocabulary of chess. A player who recognises a fork, a pin, or a discovered attack can translate calculation into points at the board. Know these patterns — and know them cold.

Fundamental Patterns

Tactic

The Fork Beginner

A single piece attacks two or more enemy pieces simultaneously. The knight is the most infamous forking piece — its L-shaped leap creates threats that are often invisible until it's too late.

Key piece: Knight, Queen, Pawn
Learn More →
Tactic

The Pin Beginner

A piece cannot move without exposing a more valuable piece behind it. An absolute pin immobilises a piece in front of the king; a relative pin is one from which escape is merely costly.

Key piece: Bishop, Rook, Queen
Learn More →
Tactic

The Skewer Beginner

The pin's more violent cousin. A valuable piece is attacked and forced to move — exposing a lesser piece behind it to capture. Think of it as a reversed pin.

Key piece: Bishop, Rook, Queen
Learn More →
Tactic

Discovered Attack Intermediate

A piece moves, uncovering an attack from a piece behind it. The moving piece can itself make a threat — creating a devastating double attack that is nearly impossible to meet.

Effect: Two threats at once
Learn More →
Tactic

Discovered Check Intermediate

A discovered attack where the newly revealed threat is check. The moving piece can capture, threaten, or wreak havoc anywhere — the king must answer the check first.

Effect: Check + free move
Learn More →
Tactic

Double Check Intermediate

Both the moving piece and the piece behind it give check simultaneously. The only legal response is to move the king — it cannot be blocked or interposed. Often immediately decisive.

Effect: King must move

Advanced Patterns

Advanced Tactic

Zwischenzug Advanced

German for "in-between move." Rather than responding to a threat directly, a player inserts a more powerful intermediate move first — often a check or a counter-threat that changes everything.

Effect: Disrupts expected sequence
Learn More →
Advanced Tactic

Deflection Advanced

A piece guarding a key square or piece is lured away — often by a sacrifice — leaving its charge unprotected. The decoy is similar but draws a piece to a disadvantageous square.

Effect: Remove the guardian
Learn More →
Advanced Tactic

Interference Advanced

A piece is interposed between two of the opponent's pieces to cut the communication between them. One of the two is then left undefended and can be captured profitably.

Effect: Break coordination
Learn More →
Advanced Tactic

Overloading Advanced

A piece is doing too much — defending two different squares or pieces at once. By attacking both targets in sequence, you force it to abandon one of its duties.

Effect: Overtax a defender
Learn More →
Advanced Tactic

X-Ray Attack Advanced

A long-range piece (bishop, rook, or queen) exerts influence through an enemy piece. When that piece moves, the piece behind it is captured. A subtle but powerful pressure.

Effect: See-through attack
Learn More →
Advanced Tactic

Zugzwang Advanced

German for "compulsion to move." Any move the player makes worsens their position. Most common in king and pawn endgames, zugzwang can decide games at the highest level.

Effect: Any move loses

Sacrificial Attacks

Sacrifice

Greek Gift Sacrifice Intermediate

White sacrifices the bishop on h7, driving the king into the open and launching a coordinated attack with knight and queen. One of the most famous and frequently occurring combinations in club chess.

Pattern: Bxh7+ Kxh7 Ng5+
Learn More →
Sacrifice

Exchange Sacrifice Advanced

Surrendering a rook for a minor piece to gain positional compensation — often an outpost, a powerful bishop pair, or a lasting initiative. Tal and Petrosian both mastered this art in very different styles.

Pattern: Rxc3 bxc3 — structural gain
Learn More →
Sacrifice

Queen Sacrifice Advanced

The most dramatic of all sacrifices. Giving up the queen for two minor pieces or a rook plus pawn is sometimes correct — but the decision demands precise calculation and an unflinching nerve.

Effect: Material for initiative
Learn More →
Sacrifice

The Hook Mate Intermediate

A rook, knight, and pawn combine in a classic mating pattern on the side of the board. The opponent's own pawn hooks the king in, leaving no escape. Appears frequently in middlegame attacks.

Pattern: Rh8# with Ng6 + h-pawn
Learn More →
Sacrifice

Clearance Sacrifice Advanced

A piece is sacrificed not to win material but purely to vacate a square or open a file for a more powerful piece. The combination works because what comes next is unstoppable.

Effect: Clear the path
Learn More →
Sacrifice

Pawn Breakthrough Intermediate

In an endgame or advanced middlegame position, a pawn sacrifice blows open a blocked pawn chain, creating a passed pawn that the opponent cannot stop. A technique every player must master.

Effect: Create passed pawn

Checkmating Patterns

Checkmate

Back Rank Mate Beginner

The king is trapped on the back rank by its own pawns. A rook or queen sweeps in on the first or eighth rank. One of the most common mating motifs at club level.

Learn More →
Checkmate

Scholar's Mate Beginner

A four-move checkmate targeting the f7 square. Every beginner must know how to execute and — more importantly — how to defend against it confidently.

Learn More →
Checkmate

Smothered Mate Intermediate

The king is surrounded ("smothered") by its own pieces and checkmated by a knight. The setup typically involves a sacrifice to drive the king to the corner first.

Learn More →
Checkmate

Anastasia's Mate Intermediate

A rook and knight combine to trap the king on the edge of the board. The knight controls the squares the rook cannot reach. Elegant and often surprising in practical play.

Learn More →
Checkmate

Arabian Mate Intermediate

A rook cuts off the king's escape along a rank or file; the knight delivers the fatal blow. One of the oldest and most beautiful checkmating patterns in chess.

Learn More →
Checkmate

Boden's Mate Advanced

Two criss-crossing bishops deliver checkmate to a king tangled up in its own pieces. Often arises from a queen sacrifice to open lines for the bishops.

Endgame Tactics

Endgame Tactic

Stalemate Trick Intermediate

The defender, facing inevitable loss, sacrifices remaining material to leave the king with no legal moves. A resourceful trick that saves countless lost positions — and one every attacker must guard against at all times.

Defensive resource: Induce stalemate
Learn More →
Endgame Tactic

Underpromotion Advanced

Instead of promoting a pawn to a queen, a player chooses a rook, bishop, or knight. Underpromotion to a knight is the most common — it can give check that a queen cannot, creating a fork or mating net.

Effect: Unexpected piece, new threat
Learn More →
Endgame Tactic

Shouldering Intermediate

The attacking king uses geometry to block the defending king from reaching a critical square, effectively "shouldering" it aside. A pure king manoeuvre that does not involve captures — subtle and decisive.

Effect: King cuts off king
Learn More →
Endgame Tactic

Triangulation Advanced

The king moves in a triangle to lose a tempo, transferring the obligation to move to the opponent and placing them in zugzwang. Requires more than one tempo — only possible when the opponent's king cannot mirror the path.

Effect: Lose a tempo deliberately
Learn More →
Endgame Tactic

Rook Skewer in Endings Intermediate

In rook endings, attacking the king forces it to step aside, allowing capture of the pawn behind it. More complex than in the middlegame — the endgame context makes timing and king position critical.

Key piece: Rook
Learn More →
Endgame Tactic

Fortress Construction Advanced

The weaker side builds an impregnable defensive formation that cannot be broken even with overwhelming material advantage. The classic bishop-and-rook-pawn vs. wrong-coloured bishop draw is the most famous fortress in chess.

Effect: Draw from material deficit

Tactic of the Week

This Week

Greek Gift Sacrifice

White opens with Bxh7+. The king steps forward, Ng5+ follows, and the queen storms to h5. The defence has just three moves and each is losing. A textbook kingside destruction.

Pattern: Bxh7+ Kxh7 Ng5+ Kg8 Qh5

Train Your Tactics

Consistent daily practice is the fastest way to improve. Aim for at least 15 minutes of tactical puzzles every day. Patterns become instincts only through repetition.

Recommended resources: Lichess Puzzles, Chess Tempo, and the classic 1001 Chess Exercises for Club Players by Frank Erwich.

Endgame Tactic Quick Guide

Stalemate: Always check if opponent can induce it.
Underpromotion: Consider knight promotion for forks.
Triangulation: Requires king on a different-coloured square cycle.
Fortress: Identify drawing fortresses before giving up.
