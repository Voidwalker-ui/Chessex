Carlsen Crushes Gukesh in a Model Queen Endgame

Magnus Carlsen 1 – 0 D. Gukesh

The world's greatest endgame technician demonstrates once more why queen endings remain his private territory.

Magnus Carlsen entered the game with a single point lead at Norway Chess and needed nothing more than a solid performance. He delivered something far more compelling. From a queenless middlegame that appeared heading toward a swift draw, Carlsen began a patient king march that Gukesh — despite playing with commendable accuracy — could not sufficiently counter. The endgame that followed was a 58-move exhibition of the type of chess that cannot be imitated: technically impeccable, psychologically relentless, and beautiful in the way that only technique can be beautiful.

The critical moment arrived on move 34, when Carlsen placed his king on e5. The square had been prepared twelve moves earlier. Gukesh had no target to attack, no weakness to exploit, and no counter-plan to speak of. From that point, the game moved inevitably toward its conclusion. Carlsen converted with the quiet precision that has defined his career for twenty years.

The opening — a Nimzo-Indian Defence, Classical Variation — produced the kind of unbalanced pawn structure Carlsen routinely turns into endgame weapons. The bishop pair, a slight space advantage on the queenside, and a passed d-pawn were enough. They are always enough in his hands.

May 2025  ·  Match Report · Candidates Tournament, Toronto

Firouzja's Sacrifice Stuns Nepomniachtchi in Najdorf Thriller

Ian Nepomniachtchi 0 – 1 Alireza Firouzja

A Najdorf position of maximum sharpness — and the young Frenchman chose the most aggressive path through it.

The Candidates Tournament in Toronto produced no more violent game than round nine's encounter between Nepomniachtchi and Firouzja. Both players had entered the round with winning ambitions; only one left satisfied. Firouzja, playing Black in a Sicilian Najdorf, uncorked a piece sacrifice on move 22 that had all the hallmarks of deep preparation. The rook was surrendered on c3, the pawn structure shattered, and suddenly White's centre — seemingly dominant just five moves earlier — became the source of a lethal attack rather than the engine of victory.

Nepomniachtchi spent 41 minutes on his 24th move. He found the best defence available. It was not enough. Firouzja's attack moved with the efficiency of a prepared variation — every piece reaching its optimal square in sequence, every defensive resource pre-calculated and dismissed. By move 38, resignation came quietly, with three pawns left on the board and no path to survival.

The game is destined for the anthologies. Its move-22 sacrifice joins a lineage of Najdorf brilliancies stretching back through Fischer, Tal, and Polugaevsky — attacks so sharp that even engines require extended analysis time to confirm their soundness.

April 2025  ·  Match Report · Superbet Classic, Bucharest

Pragg Holds a Masterful Berlin Draw Against Caruana

R. Praggnanandhaa ½ – ½ Fabiano Caruana

At 19, the Indian prodigy demonstrated a depth of defensive understanding that belies his age.

Fabiano Caruana is among the most dangerous classical players in the world when he reaches a Berlin endgame with even the slightest structural advantage. Against Praggnanandhaa in Bucharest, he had exactly that: a slightly superior king position, an outside passed a-pawn, and the initiative. It was the sort of position where most players would find themselves slowly squeezed. Praggnanandhaa found no fewer than three successive drawing resources — each more precise than the last — to hold a draw that felt, by the final handshake, like a victory.

The key was the Indian's understanding of the "wrong rook's pawn" principle. On move 47, he was able to enter a position where Caruana's a-pawn, despite its advanced position, could not be escorted home without allowing a fortress. The rook triangulation that followed — passive to the eye but deeply active in conception — secured the half-point in twenty more moves of absolute precision.

It was the kind of defensive display that rarely earns applause, but every serious player watching understood what they had witnessed.

The Anatomy of a Breakthrough: How to Destroy a Pawn Chain

Every pawn chain has a base. Every base is a target. The art lies in identifying it before your opponent fortifies it.

Nimzowitsch's theory of pawn chains remains one of the most practical analytical tools in chess. A pawn chain — a diagonal formation of pawns each protected by the one behind it — is only as strong as its most backward member. That member is the base, and the base is the target. This is not merely theory. It is a plan.

The French Defence builds a pawn chain around d5–e6. White's typical response, e5, creates the opposing chain e5–d4 with its base on d4. Black's correct plan, according to Nimzowitsch, is c5 — attacking the base. White's correct plan is f4 and then f5, advancing the chain rather than defending it. The resulting middlegame is not a matter of local skirmishes; it is two armies attacking at opposite ends of a connected structure. One will reach the base first. That player wins the pawn structure battle, and often the game.

Understanding this in the abstract is one thing. Applying it under time pressure, three hours into a game, when a dozen candidate moves compete for attention — that is the work of study and practice. The patterns must become automatic before they become useful.

What the Opening Tells You About the Endgame

The choices made in the first ten moves cast long shadows over the final phase of the game.

Chess is not three separate games glued together. It is one continuous narrative, and the opening is its first chapter. A player who reaches a rook endgame without understanding why has not played chess — they have survived it. The great Capablanca said one must study the endgame before anything else. Yet for most club players, the opening is where passion lives: the Italian Game, the Dragon, the King's Indian. These names conjure romance, aggression, subtlety. The endgame, by contrast, feels barren — spare and technical. This view is a mistake. The endgame is where opening ideas are vindicated or punished. A Catalan player who reaches a queen endgame with an outside passed pawn is not lucky — they planned it from move three.

April 14, 2025  ·  History

The Match of the Century: Fischer vs. Spassky, Reykjavik 1972

More than a chess match — it was a Cold War drama played over 64 squares under the watchful eye of the world.

Bobby Fischer arrived late to almost everything in Reykjavik. To the opening ceremony. To the first game — which he forfeited. To the second game, which he forfeited again. Yet when he finally sat down to play, he did so with such calculated ferocity that Boris Spassky, the reigning world champion, applauded one of his opponent's moves from across the board. Game 6 is considered by many to be the greatest single game of chess ever played. Fischer's queenside pawn majority, his bishop manoeuvre, his quiet confidence — it was chess played as if it were already history. Spassky never recovered. Fischer won the match 12½–8½ and became the first American world chess champion.

Prophylaxis: The Art of Preventing What Has Not Yet Happened

The greatest players don't merely respond to threats — they prevent them from arising in the first place.

Petrosian was called "Iron Tigran" not because he attacked with ferocity but because nothing ever attacked him. His genius lay in a concept that separates strong players from exceptional ones: prophylaxis — the ability to think on behalf of your opponent, to sense their ambitions, and to quietly neutralise them before they take form. Most players think forward: what can I do? The prophylactic thinker asks instead: what does my opponent want, and how do I stop them? This is not passive chess. It is chess played at a deeper level — strategic, anticipatory, suffocating. By the time your opponent realises their plan is impossible, the initiative has already passed to you.

March 12, 2025  ·  Player Profile

Mikhail Tal: The Magician from Riga

A world champion who played chess not as a science but as a dark art — with sacrifices that defied both calculation and refutation.

Mikhail Tal became world champion in 1960 at just 23 years old, and he did so by playing chess that made grandmasters doubt their own sanity. His sacrifices were often objectively unsound. He knew this. He also knew that the complications they generated were beyond human calculation — and he was better than anyone at navigating chaos. "You must take your opponent into a deep, dark forest," Tal once said, "where two plus two equals five, and the path leading out is only wide enough for one." Tal's games remain the most exciting in chess history. They are not always correct. They are always alive.

February 25, 2025  ·  Improvement

Why You Should Study Grandmaster Games — Not Just Puzzles

Tactics puzzles build pattern recognition. But it is the study of complete games that builds chess understanding.

The modern chess improvement ecosystem is dominated by puzzles. Platforms generate thousands of them; apps award streaks and trophies for completing them. This is not without value — tactical pattern recognition is genuinely important. But puzzles are excerpts. They strip away the context in which combinations arise. The position is already sharp; the tactic is already present. You need only find it. Real chess requires you to create the sharpness. To understand why the pieces were arranged that way — what strategic and tactical decisions led to a position where a combination was possible — you must study the whole game. Specifically, the games of those who understood chess most deeply.
