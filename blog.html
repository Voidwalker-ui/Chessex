What the Opening Tells You About the Endgame

The choices made in the first ten moves cast long shadows over the final phase of the game.

Chess is not three separate games glued together. It is one continuous narrative, and the opening is its first chapter. A player who reaches a rook endgame without understanding why has not played chess — they have survived it. The great Capablanca said one must study the endgame before anything else. Yet for most club players, the opening is where passion lives: the Italian Game, the Dragon, the King's Indian. These names conjure romance, aggression, subtlety. The endgame, by contrast, feels barren — spare and technical. This view is a mistake. The endgame is where opening ideas are vindicated or punished. A Catalan player who reaches a queen endgame with an outside passed pawn is not lucky — they planned it from move three.

The Match of the Century: Fischer vs. Spassky, Reykjavik 1972

More than a chess match — it was a Cold War drama played over 64 squares under the watchful eye of the world.

Bobby Fischer arrived late to almost everything in Reykjavik. To the opening ceremony. To the first game — which he forfeited. To the second game, which he forfeited again. Yet when he finally sat down to play, he did so with such calculated ferocity that Boris Spassky, the reigning world champion, applauded one of his opponent's moves from across the board. Game 6 is considered by many to be the greatest single game of chess ever played. Fischer's queenside pawn majority, his bishop manoeuvre, his quiet confidence — it was chess played as if it were already history. Spassky never recovered. Fischer won the match 12½–8½ and became the first American world chess champion.

Prophylaxis: The Art of Preventing What Has Not Yet Happened

The greatest players don't merely respond to threats — they prevent them from arising in the first place.

Petrosian was called "Iron Tigran" not because he attacked with ferocity but because nothing ever attacked him. His genius lay in a concept that separates strong players from exceptional ones: prophylaxis — the ability to think on behalf of your opponent, to sense their ambitions, and to quietly neutralise them before they take form. Most players think forward: what can I do? The prophylactic thinker asks instead: what does my opponent want, and how do I stop them? This is not passive chess. It is chess played at a deeper level — strategic, anticipatory, suffocating. By the time your opponent realises their plan is impossible, the initiative has already passed to you.

Mikhail Tal: The Magician from Riga

A world champion who played chess not as a science but as a dark art — with sacrifices that defied both calculation and refutation.

Mikhail Tal became world champion in 1960 at just 23 years old, and he did so by playing chess that made grandmasters doubt their own sanity. His sacrifices were often objectively unsound. He knew this. He also knew that the complications they generated were beyond human calculation — and he was better than anyone at navigating chaos. "You must take your opponent into a deep, dark forest," Tal once said, "where two plus two equals five, and the path leading out is only wide enough for one." Tal's games remain the most exciting in chess history. They are not always correct. They are always alive.

Why You Should Study Grandmaster Games — Not Just Puzzles

Tactics puzzles build pattern recognition. But it is the study of complete games that builds chess understanding.

The modern chess improvement ecosystem is dominated by puzzles. Platforms generate thousands of them; apps award streaks and trophies for completing them. This is not without value — tactical pattern recognition is genuinely important. But puzzles are excerpts. They strip away the context in which combinations arise. The position is already sharp; the tactic is already present. You need only find it. Real chess requires you to create the sharpness. To understand why the pieces were arranged that way — what strategic and tactical decisions led to a position where a combination was possible — you must study the whole game. Specifically, the games of those who understood chess most deeply.
