Featured

What the Opening Tells You About the Endgame

The choices made in the first ten moves cast long shadows over the final phase of the game — a truth that separates masters from amateurs.

Chess is not three separate games glued together. It is one continuous narrative, and the opening is its first chapter. A player who reaches a rook endgame without understanding why has not played chess — they have survived it.

The great Capablanca said one must study the endgame before anything else. Yet for most club players, the opening is where passion lives: the Italian Game, the Dragon, the King's Indian. These names conjure romance, aggression, subtlety. The endgame, by contrast, feels barren — spare and technical.

This view is a mistake. The endgame is where opening ideas are vindicated or punished. A Catalan player who reaches a queen endgame with an outside passed pawn is not lucky — they planned it from move three.
